2020 is going to be a landmark year for data protection in CA when the state’s own data privacy act, CCPA (California Consumer Protection Act), goes into effect on January 1. CCPA makes it mandatory for organizations to treat data protection as a business priority, requiring transparency about personal consumer information collected or sold/shared by businesses, and empowering consumers not only with control over the collection and sharing of their personal information but also with the right to sue for data breaches when their personal information is compromised. It incentivizes business to strengthen the security safeguards to protect personal information, including reviewing the security of the vendors they use to provide service
CCPA significantly raises the stakes for vendor security, because we are now even more accountable to both our customers and their consumers for any lapses in our vendors’ security practices. As explained in December 2018 issue of the Mode Security newsletter, we have a vendor assessment policy which requires that before starting to use a new vendor, you should always request a vendor security review. This not only helps us ensure that the security practices of the vendor are evaluated but also to adequately protect ourselves in case of a vendor security breach. If we start using a vendor that does not go through the vendor review process, we would not be able to take appropriate measures to protect ourselves and therefore expose Mode and our customers to risk of additional legal liability.
Security breaches are not always the work of external hackers, but can in fact occur because of internal process flaws, such as software changes that get introduced without proper review. Continuing the discussion we started in April 2019 issue of the Mode Security newsletter, here are additional high profile examples when unreviewed code has led to avoidable security mistakes.
Companies must pay careful attention to APIs that process sensitive data so that it is not inappropriately disclosed. Twitter learnt this lesson the hard way when it accidentally disclosed location data for some of its users. More specifically, Twitter accidentally collected location data when a user did not enable precise location sharing feature. Similarly, Facebook put the data of about 50M users at risk when it inappropriately disclosed access tokens of users that could be used to take over their Facebook accounts. This resulted due to a combination of bugs affecting the View As feature and the video uploader service.
Once again, the moral of the story is that we should review software changes for unintended security consequences. At Mode, we have a Security Reviews policy which outlines a security review criteria to evaluate any design and code changes for security consequences. The Security and Product teams work closely to review the pipeline of projects underway and flag scenarios for security review that meet the criteria. Both teams also conduct a regular security triage to make sure any security vulnerabilities identified in the product are addressed in a timely manner according to our established protocol.
At Mode, we have a responsibility to protect the data of our customers and our company. As guardians of sensitive customer and company information, each of us should always be vigilant to protect this information from unauthorized access and compromise. This includes securing our laptops, which is part of our security obligations as per the Mode Workplace Security Policy.
As part of on-boarding, all Mode laptops are configured with secure defaults, which include enabling FileVault encryption, and lock screen to automatically turn on at 5 minutes. As an additional precaution, our policy recommends that when you temporarily step away from your laptop, please ensure that you lock the laptop screen so that non-employees cannot access confidential information. You can create a shortcut for doing that by adding a lock screen icon to Mac Touch Bar. Furthermore, you should always make sure never to leave your laptop attended in public places, or in the office when no other Mode employees are present (so that non-employees do not have an opportunity to grab it). Lastly, please choose a strong password for your laptop, so that it is unlikely for someone to compromise your account in the event that your laptop is lost or stolen. All these small details go a long way in helping all of us keeping our data secure.
